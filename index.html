How It Came About

It started the way every beach day did: a mountain of gear — cooler, chairs, umbrella, toys, boogie boards — and a long, sweaty drag across soft sand that swallowed every wheel I threw at it. There had to be a better way, so I set out to build one.

What began as "just haul the stuff" became a real engineering project — one built for the real world, not a workbench. The frame is hand-welded two-inch aluminum, sized to haul roughly 500–600 lbs of gear across the sand without blinking. Big balloon tires float over loose sand instead of plowing into it. Two electric motors and a closed-loop speed controller deliver smooth, steady power up the dunes, and a Raspberry Pi serves as the brain that ties it all together.

The fun was in the problem-solving. Nearly every part of this cart is something I designed, printed, wired, or wrote from scratch — and almost every step meant hitting a wall and engineering my way through it: dialing in the drivetrain so it eases in smoothly instead of lurching, building custom power systems so sensitive electronics stay rock-solid right next to high-current motors, 3D-modeling brackets and enclosures tough enough to survive sand and sun, and tuning it all one prototype at a time until it simply worked.

Then it got ambitious. I added a small wearable tag and four sensors on the cart's corners so it can pinpoint exactly where I am and drive itself right to me — completely hands-free — while a spinning 360° scanner and a ring of sensors steer it around people and stop it cold at curbs and drop-offs. Feel like driving instead? Flip a switch on the remote and take over in an instant.

That's KMJ Dynamics: top-notch engineering aimed squarely at a real-world need — a cart tough enough for the job, smart enough to carry its own weight, and built to make beach days effortless.

Power Electronics

Built to survive its own motors

Early on, a single voltage spike wiped out the main computer. So I re-engineered the entire power system: distributed, individually-regulated supplies and dedicated surge protection that keep delicate control electronics rock-steady sitting inches from high-current motors. Failure became the spec.

Real-Time Control

Smooth, not lurching

Drive and steering run on the Raspberry Pi 5's dedicated hardware timers — not jittery software signals — with slew-rate limiting and closed-loop PID speed control. The result is silky, predictable motion that eases in and out of power instead of jerking, even under a full load of gear.

Autonomy

Map-free navigation

A wearable radio beacon fixes exactly where you are while a spinning 360° laser scanner and a "follow-the-gap" algorithm let the cart flow around people and obstacles and then resume its line to you. No pre-built maps and no GPS — it simply senses and reacts, in real time.

Telemetry

Watch it from anywhere

Battery voltage, current and state-of-charge are read straight off the motor controllers over an industrial CAN bus, then streamed — alongside a live obstacle radar — to a phone dashboard over a private, encrypted network. Check on the cart from across the beach or across the country.

Patented Engineering

KMJ Dynamics is more than the beach cart. Its founder, John Maslowski, is the sole inventor on a family of granted U.S. patents for a novel electrical power system.

The invention is a compact, emission-free power system: a battery-driven motor turns a single shaft that spins two generators at once — a primary generator that delivers standard AC power to your devices, and a secondary generator that feeds charge back to the battery. The result is a clean, quiet alternative to fuel-burning generators, designed to run indoors and off-grid.

It's the same first-principles engineering that drives the autonomous beach cart — aimed at a different problem.
