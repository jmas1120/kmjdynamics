How It Came About

It started the way every beach day did: a mountain of gear — cooler, chairs, umbrella, toys, boogie boards — and a long, sweaty drag across soft sand that swallowed every wheel I threw at it. There had to be a better way, so I set out to build one.

What began as "just haul the stuff" became a real engineering project — one built for the real world, not a workbench. The frame is hand-welded two-inch aluminum, sized to haul roughly 500–600 lbs of gear across the sand without blinking. Big balloon tires float over loose sand instead of plowing into it. Two electric motors and a closed-loop speed controller deliver smooth, steady power up the dunes, and a Raspberry Pi serves as the brain that ties it all together.

The fun was in the problem-solving. Nearly every part of this cart is something I designed, printed, wired, or wrote from scratch — and almost every step meant hitting a wall and engineering my way through it: dialing in the drivetrain so it eases in smoothly instead of lurching, building custom power systems so sensitive electronics stay rock-solid right next to high-current motors, 3D-modeling brackets and enclosures tough enough to survive sand and sun, and tuning it all one prototype at a time until it simply worked.

Then it got ambitious. I added a small wearable tag and four sensors on the cart's corners so it can pinpoint exactly where I am and drive itself right to me — completely hands-free — while a spinning 360° scanner and a ring of sensors steer it around people and stop it cold at curbs and drop-offs. Feel like driving instead? Flip a switch on the remote and take over in an instant.

That's KMJ Dynamics: top-notch engineering aimed squarely at a real-world need — a cart tough enough for the job, smart enough to carry its own weight, and built to make beach days effortless.
